Meet the Board Tour

2017

Sunday, August 13 from 12-5pm



Tucker Garden
802 NW 116th St Seattle WA 98177

Fourteen years ago, Shelagh Tucker transformed her water-hungry front lawn into a xeric masterpiece brimming with a bounty of drought-tolerant gems. Inspired by Beth Chatto’s “The Gravel Garden”, Shelagh commissioned renowned local designer Phil Wood to create a garden that will thrive in Seattle’s dry summers while still looking good in winter. Mature specimens of Eucalptus archeri, Arctostaphyllos viscida, and Acacia pravissima add to the beauty and structure of the garden. The backyard evokes more of a Victorian ethos, with a glass conservatory, goldfish pond, and intently maintained perennial borders.



Livingston Garden
13021 12th Ave NW Seattle, WA 98177

Though Millie Livingston had very little gardening experience when she first began to tackle her garden 28 years ago, the physical features of the property, the sloped geography, the Puget Sound views, the towering trees and its wildness completely captivated her. The hardscape design and plant choices have been nurtured by a collaboration of professionals whose artful vision, practical knowledge and hard work helped create this unique place. Towering fir trees provide shade for woodland gardens, and the terraced spaces offer views of Puget Sound. An outdoor granite fireplace, an intricately tiled vegetable garden, and a Japanese Teahouse overlooking a shaded gully all contribute to a garden unlike any other.

Please note: Seeing all of this garden will require traversing over 100 steps. If you have any difficulty going up or down steps without handrails, we suggest enjoying the other five gardens in lieu of this one.



Kunnen Garden
19334 8th Ave NW Shoreline, Wa 98177

Towering Embothrium coccineum, Acer griseum, and Trochodendron aralioides signal you’ve just entered the garden of a longtime passionate plant collector. Created by Jeanette Kunnen, this 44-year-old garden is a tapestry of the most rare and unusual plants growing from every corner of the earth. Jeanette continually edits the garden to make space for the tiny and unglamorous as well as the newly introduced and different. Once a flat, sterile, and uninspiring city lot, Jeanette’s garden beautifully concatenates a collection of living memories of far off places once visited and enjoyed or dreamed of with places she has yet to explore.

Please note: Parking is limited to street parking only. We reccommend parking on 195th or 193rd streets and walking to the garden from there. There is also space in the Heritage Bank parking lot (1/3 of a mile away). Please do not park on sidewalks or block mailboxes or driveways. Those with walking challenges may park in the driveway (space permitting).



Amundson Garden
18454 16th Ave NW Shoreline, WA 98177

Twenty-five years ago, JoAnn and Bruce Amundson set out to transform their half-acre property into a garden showcasing their sculpture collection. Their vision was to create a landscape enhanced by monumental sculptures, and likewise, ensure their art collection enhanced the garden itself. As you enter the Amundson garden, you’ll find a sign displaying their motto: “Great art teaches us to live life more alertly." Bounded by a year-round creek, this natural, northwest-style garden is a marriage of nature and contemporary art — now one of the largest collections of outdoor sculptures in the Puget Sound area.



Kruckeberg Botanic Garden
20312 15th Ave NW, Shoreline, WA 98177

This four-acre public garden contains a unique blend of Pacific Northwest native plants and unusual exotics set in a naturalistic wooded setting. During your visit you'll see more than 2,000 species, which include native and exotic conifers, hardwoods, rhododendrons, magnolias, ferns and groundcovers. Several trees are the largest or most rare in the state. Birdwatchers have identified more than 40 bird species while visiting the Garden.



Thompson Garden
720 Spruce Street Edmonds, WA 98020

Walter Thompson’s backyard oasis places the small, charming, and familiar right next to the big, bold, and unusual. A majestic display of 'Mt Loma Prieta Spike' redwoods leads to a bridge crossing over a natural stream running through the garden. The sound of restless water spilling into two very old concrete ponds adds a generous dose of enchantment. As you meander through the garden’s winding paths and bridges, you’ll find a variety of rhododendrons and maples along with some tender exotics that spend the winter in an old glasshouse. A wooden summer house built in the 1930s also adds a bit of charm and history to the garden.
